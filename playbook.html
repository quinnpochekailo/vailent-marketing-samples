Chapter One
01

The Moment.

Why commercial leaders at material companies cannot sit out AI in 2026, and why the bottleneck is not the technology.

You already know the headline. In every industry adjacent to yours (software, financial services, logistics, even consumer packaged goods), AI has moved from a curiosity on the CEO's desk to a line item on the commercial team's roadmap. For a long time, material industries were the safe place to wait it out. Specialty chemistry, polymer formulation, elastomer compounding, rubber processing. These are technical businesses with long sales cycles, deeply embedded customer relationships, and operating systems that have run on Excel and email for two decades. Nothing about that environment screams "early adopter."

That window is closing faster than the trade press suggests, and it is closing from the buyer side first. ICIS research on chemical distribution puts numbers behind what every commercial leader has already noticed in their own pipeline.

Buyers are not waiting for the industry to digitize. They are choosing the suppliers and distributors that already have.

The Shift in One Sentence

Your buyers' purchasing behavior has already changed. The question is whether your commercial operation has changed with it, or whether the change is showing up in your win-rate data first.

The pressure on the cost side is just as real. McKinsey's analysis of digitization in chemical distribution finds that cost-to-serve can fall 15% to 50% through workflow digitization and AI-assisted commercial operations. A VP of Commercial Excellence looking at a spreadsheet-driven team carrying 5% to 10% gross margins on commodity products and 25% to 40% on specialty cannot ignore a structural cost line that large. The reps cannot work harder. The pricing analysts cannot version-control more spreadsheets. The path forward is a different operating system, not a heavier one.

Here is the harder reality. AI adoption in commercial operations is not a "the CEO decided" decision the way an enterprise resource planning, or ERP, swap is. It is a function-level decision — yours. You own the quoting cycle. You own pricing governance. You own pipeline visibility and key-account growth. The same way the VP of Sales owned the rollout of customer relationship management software, or CRM, in the 2010s, the VP of Commercial Excellence owns the rollout of AI in commercial operations in 2026. The bottleneck on AI adoption inside your function is not the technology. It is whether the person reading this page decides to move.

This Playbook is written for that decision. Not a vendor pitch — a framework you can take into your next leadership team meeting: what AI actually means for commercial operations, the five highest-return places to point it first, a 90-day path to a first proof point, and the pitfalls that derail most adoption programs.

Chapter Two
02

What “AI in Commercial Ops” Actually Means.

Strip away the marketing language. AI in a material-industry commercial function comes down to a small number of concrete things.

It is not a chatbot. It is not a content generator. It is not a science-fiction agent that runs your business autonomously. It is a set of capabilities that sit on top of the data your team already produces (quotes, orders, technical data sheets, or TDSs; certificates of analysis, or COAs; safety data sheets, or SDSs; CRM notes; ERP records) and removes the manual labor that currently turns that data into a usable commercial answer.

In plain terms, AI in commercial operations means five things, and you can evaluate any vendor by whether their product delivers them:

  • Faster quoting. Pulling product specifications, pricing rules, freight, and margin guardrails into a quote that used to take a rep hours to assemble.
  • Pricing governance. Enforcing pricing policy across thousands of customer-by-stock-keeping-unit combinations without the rep having to remember a single rule.
  • Pipeline and key-account visibility. A real view of what is moving, what is stuck, and which accounts are quietly under-bought relative to their potential.
  • Customer onboarding. Compressing the know-your-customer, or KYC, checks, credit reviews, and compliance verification that today turn a new buyer into a two-week waiting game.
  • Document and data orchestration. Putting the COAs, TDSs, and SDSs your buyers ask for into reach the moment they ask, in the format they need.

None of that is theoretical. All of it is being done today by software the same way payroll or accounts payable are done by software, with a human in the loop where judgment matters, and automation where it does not. The reason this is now a category-defining moment is that large language models, or LLMs, have made it possible to do this on top of the messy, unstructured data that material industries actually run on. Specifications written in PDF. Pricing rules scattered across spreadsheets. Customer notes living in a sales rep's inbox. The previous generation of commercial software needed clean, structured data and almost no material company had any. The current generation does not.

Chapter Three
03

Five High-ROI Use Cases for Commercial Leaders.

Ordered by speed-to-proof, not by ultimate value. Pick one. Prove it. Then expand.

If you are going to move on AI in the next twelve months, here is where to point it first. The fastest measurable win is quoting cycle time. The largest long-term win is key-account expansion. Either way, the principle holds: pick one, prove it, then expand.

01
Compress the Quoting Cycle
Pain
Quoting in chemical distribution is a seven-step manual process. A request that should take five minutes routinely takes hours, sometimes days. Reps re-derive freight, dig up margin floors, and hand-key specs.
Solution
AI pulls customer history, SKU rules, pricing guardrails, freight, and product documents into a draft quote the rep edits rather than authors.
Outcome
Quote turnaround compressed from hours to minutes. Rep capacity reclaimed for higher-value selling. Win-rate lift tracks closely to first-quote speed.
02
Govern Pricing at Scale
Pain
500 customers by 1,000 stock-keeping units is half a million pricing scenarios. Most live in spreadsheets with no version control, no audit trail, no enforcement. Margin erodes one quote at a time.
Solution
AI enforces pricing policy at the moment of quote, flags out-of-policy deals before they leave the rep's screen, surfaces margin drift across customers and segments.
Outcome
Recovery of margin that today bleeds out through inconsistent enforcement. A defensible pricing process the CFO can actually audit.
03
Bring Pipeline Out of the Spreadsheet
Pain
Roughly 40% of chemical sales reps still run their pipeline in spreadsheets and email. Many companies have no CRM in real operational use. Forecast accuracy is whatever the VP can talk reps into on Friday.
Solution
AI captures pipeline activity automatically from email and quoting, classifies stage, produces a forecast view without depending on rep data hygiene.
Outcome
A pipeline that reflects reality rather than a rep's best guess. Forecast credibility with the CFO. A leading indicator the VP can manage to.
04
Shrink Customer Onboarding
Pain
KYC, anti-money-laundering checks, credit reviews, and compliance verification turn a willing new buyer into a two-week wait. In a market where 85% of buyers prefer digital, that delay is churn risk before the relationship begins.
Solution
AI orchestrates document collection, compliance lookups, and approval routing in parallel rather than sequentially.
Outcome
New-account time-to-first-order shrunk by 60%+ in the realistic case. A buyer who experiences the relationship as fast from day one.
05
Find the Untouched Revenue Inside Your Key Accounts
Pain
Every commercial leader knows the largest source of new revenue is the customer that already buys. Every commercial leader also knows the rep does not have time to systematically work the catalog against the customer's actual application portfolio. The opportunity stays invisible.
Solution
AI matches the customer's buying pattern, technical profile, and adjacent industry usage against the catalog to surface specific cross-sell openings — the SKUs the customer is not yet buying but should be, with the technical reason attached.
Outcome
A named, prioritized list of expansion plays the rep can work in a quarter. The single largest commercial-AI lift in specialty distribution, where rep coverage is the binding constraint on growth.
The Discipline

The mistake is buying the platform and trying to roll out five use cases at once. The discipline is to pick the one where your data is cleanest, your pain is sharpest, and your team is most ready, and drive it to a measurable result inside a quarter.

Chapter Four
04

The 90-Day Adoption Playbook.

Run it against a single workflow, in shadow mode, with a named milestone at the end of each phase. Prove one thing. Then scale.

Most AI adoption programs in material industries fail the same way enterprise software programs failed in the 2000s: too much scope, too long a runway, too many stakeholders, and no measurable outcome until twelve months in — at which point the executive sponsor has moved on. The fix is structural.

Three principles make this work. First, the baseline metric is non-negotiable. You cannot prove an improvement against a vibe. Second, shadow mode is the unlock: running AI alongside the human process gives you the failure-mode data you need before betting reps on it. Third, the go/no-go is real. The discipline of being willing to stop on day 90 is what makes the program credible to the rest of the executive team. A program that cannot be stopped is a program that will never get started.

“
Do not boil the ocean. Prove one workflow. Then earn the right to the next one.
Chapter Five
05

Common Pitfalls.

Most AI adoption failures in material industries are not caused by the AI. They are caused by predictable organizational patterns that derail any new system.

Four show up almost every time.

01

The Data-Quality Myth

What people say
"Our data is too messy for AI. Let's spend a year cleaning it up first."
What actually happens
The cleanup project becomes its own program with its own budget and its own slipping timeline. AI adoption stalls indefinitely. Meanwhile, the current generation of LLMs is specifically designed to work on messy, unstructured data. PDF specs, inconsistent units, hand-written customer notes. These are exactly the inputs modern systems handle.
The right move
Start with the workflow that has the worst data. Use the AI pilot to surface what is actually broken, then fix only what blocks the workflow. Data cleanup as a side effect of adoption beats data cleanup as a prerequisite to it.
02

The Replace-People Fear

What people say
"My reps will think this tool is being rolled out to replace them. Adoption will tank."
What actually happens
The reps who use the tool become two to three times more productive than the ones who do not. None of them get replaced. The team's revenue per rep simply doubles. The reps who refuse to adopt eventually self-select out, usually voluntarily, when the gap becomes visible in their commission numbers.
The right move
Position the tool as rep capacity multiplier, not rep replacement. Pair the rollout with an explicit promise: no headcount reductions in the first year. Then keep that promise.
03

The ERP-Integration Trap

What people say
"We have Datacor. Nothing will integrate with Datacor. Let's wait."
What actually happens
Datacor runs more than half of the Alliance of Chemical Distributors member businesses and 700-plus installations. Every serious Material Operations AI vendor has been asked about Datacor integration. The vendors who have a real answer are easy to identify. The ones who do not should not be on your shortlist.
The right move
Make the Datacor (or SAP) integration question the third question in your first vendor call, after "show me the product" and "who has it in production." If the answer is vague, the rest of the demo does not matter.
04

The Wait-for-Perfect Trap

What people say
"This space is moving fast. Wait six months and the tools will be better."
What actually happens
The tools will be better in six months. They will also be better six months after that. The compounding advantage of the team that started in 2026 will be a year of operational learning that the team starting in 2027 cannot buy. Adoption is not a technology decision. It is an organizational learning decision, and that clock has already started.
The right move
Start with the 90-day playbook in Chapter Four. The cost of starting is bounded. The cost of waiting is not.
